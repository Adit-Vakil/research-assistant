Rotation significantly affects gravity in two primary ways: by reducing the *effective* gravitational acceleration on the surface of a rotating body due to centrifugal force, and by causing spacetime itself to be "dragged" or influenced by the rotation of massive objects, a phenomenon known as frame-dragging, as predicted by Einstein's general theory of relativity.

## Effective Gravity on Rotating Bodies

On a rotating celestial body like Earth, the observed or "effective" acceleration due to gravity is reduced by the outward-acting centrifugal force. This effect is most pronounced at the equator, where the rotational speed and thus the centrifugal force are greatest, and diminishes to zero at the poles [G2]. For instance, the acceleration due to gravity at Earth's poles is approximately 9.832 m/s², while at the equator, it is about 9.780 m/s² [G2]. This represents a reduction in effective gravity of roughly 1/3 of a percent at the equator compared to a non-rotating scenario [G1].

The effective acceleration due to gravity (g') can be described by the formula g' = g - Rω² cos²λ, where g is the acceleration without rotation, R is the body's radius, ω is its angular velocity, and λ is the latitude [G2]. This rotational effect, which reduces effective weight, is approximately 290 times weaker than gravity itself [G3]. Beyond the direct centrifugal force, a rotating body's spin also causes it to flatten into an oblate ellipsoid. This shape means the surface at the equator is further from the center of mass than at the poles, further contributing to the variation in gravity across the surface [G4, G5].

## Frame-Dragging (Lense-Thirring Effect)

Beyond the classical effect of centrifugal force, rotation has a more profound influence on gravity as described by Albert Einstein's general theory of relativity: frame-dragging, also known as the Lense-Thirring effect [G6, G8]. This phenomenon describes how a rotating massive object "drags" or influences the fabric of spacetime around it, a gravitomagnetic effect not present in Newtonian gravity [G6, G7].

First derived by Josef Lense and Hans Thirring in 1918, frame-dragging is most significant near rapidly spinning, massive objects, such as black holes [G6, G8]. On a smaller scale, Earth's rotation causes a slow precession of the Moon's orbit, known as the geodetic effect, which is a manifestation of frame-dragging [G8]. Experimental confirmation of the Lense-Thirring effect has been achieved through precise measurements of the LAGEOS and LAGEOS 2 satellites over nearly 11 years, relying on data from GRACE satellites [G7]. The Gravity Probe B mission has also provided experimental confirmation of frame-dragging [G8].

## Gravitational Field of Rotating Masses

The gravitational field of rotating massive objects, particularly uncharged, axially symmetric black holes, is mathematically described by the Kerr metric [G11]. This metric is an exact solution to Einstein's field equations and represents a generalization of the Schwarzschild metric, which describes non-rotating bodies [G11]. Rotating black holes, which possess angular momentum, form from the gravitational collapse of spinning stars or the collision of compact objects [G12].

The Kerr metric accounts for various relativistic phenomena, including gravitational lensing, redshift, time dilation, gravitational waves, and crucially, frame-dragging [G11]. The complexity of solving equations related to the Kerr metric often necessitates numerical relativity, largely due to the intricate coupling between the black hole's rotation and perturbations, which is a direct consequence of frame-dragging [G13]. Interestingly, the faster a black hole spins, the smaller its event horizon becomes, with theoretical limits suggesting the event horizon could cease to exist at extreme rotation rates [G15].

## Bottom Line

Rotation affects gravity in two distinct ways: locally, it reduces the *effective* gravitational pull on the surface of a rotating body due to the outward centrifugal force, leading to variations based on latitude and contributing to the body's oblate shape. Globally, and more fundamentally, the rotation of massive objects warps spacetime itself, causing a "dragging" effect known as frame-dragging, a key prediction of general relativity that is most pronounced around highly dense, spinning objects like black holes.
